# AI Governance & Risk Framework

## Context Frame

I'm the CEO of Thrive Market, a membership-based online grocery company with over $500M in revenue. We've rapidly adopted AI across every function — 40+ tools, 100+ non-engineering staff on Claude, Sierra handling 68% of member contacts. But we've been moving fast without guardrails. Multiple functional leaders are independently flagging the same risks: hallucination in legal work, SOX compliance gaps, tool proliferation, "AI slop" in marketing content, and data privacy concerns with member data.

We don't have a company-wide AI governance framework. No formal review policy for AI outputs. No centralized tool approval process. No AI governance committee. Each function is making its own rules. That worked when 5 engineers were using Copilot. It doesn't work when 100+ people across Legal, Finance, Marketing, and Member Services are using AI on customer-facing and compliance-sensitive work.

I have three data files:
- `risk_incidents.csv` — 15 documented AI risk incidents across functions
- `current_policies.md` — What governance exists today (not much)
- `functional_requests.md` — What each function is asking for from a governance perspective

## The Ask

Using the three attached data files, build me:

1. **Company-wide AI governance framework** — Principles, roles, and accountability structure. This should be practical, not theoretical. It needs to work for a 800-person company where AI is already embedded in daily operations. Cover: who owns AI governance, how decisions get made, how policies get enforced, and how we avoid creating so much process that we kill the innovation advantage we've built.

2. **AI output review policy by risk tier** — Not everything needs the same level of review. A marketing social post generated by AI has different risk than an AI-drafted legal contract or an AI-processed financial transaction. Create a tiered framework: what needs mandatory human review, what needs spot-check review, and what can be fully automated. Map each function's key AI use cases to the appropriate tier.

3. **Tool approval and vetting process** — We have 40+ AI tools with no central vetting. Marketing alone uses 17. Define how new AI tools get evaluated and approved: who reviews, what criteria matter (cost, data privacy, redundancy, compliance), how long it takes, and what the fast-track path looks like for low-risk tools.

4. **AI risk register with mitigation plan** — Take the 15 incidents from the data file and extrapolate: what are the systemic risks? Build a living risk register with severity, likelihood, affected functions, current controls, and recommended mitigations. Include the risks that haven't happened yet but are predictable.

5. **AI governance committee charter** — Who sits on this committee? How often do they meet? What decisions do they own vs. what stays with functional leaders? Make it lean — this shouldn't become a bureaucratic bottleneck. Recommend a specific roster from our functional leadership.

6. **Quick-reference guide for employees** — A one-page decision tree: "I want to use AI for [X]. Do I need approval? What review is required? Where do I go?" This needs to be simple enough that a merchandising analyst or a customer service lead can use it without reading a 20-page policy document.

## Why This Matters

We're at the inflection point where the speed of AI adoption has outpaced our governance. That's normal — you move fast, then you build the guardrails. But the risks are real. A fabricated statistic already made it into a marketing deck. Legal is worried about hallucinated case citations. Finance can't automate month-end close because SOX requires human review and we haven't defined what "human review" means in an AI-assisted workflow. Member Services is routing 68% of contacts through a single AI vendor with no fallback plan.

The board is going to ask: "What's your AI risk framework?" I need to have an answer that's more than "we're working on it."

## Iteration Hooks

- "The General Counsel is worried this will slow Legal down — add a section specifically addressing how governance enables rather than blocks their work"
- "Several team leads want to be 'AI captains' in their functions — incorporate that concept into the governance structure"
- "The CFO wants cost governance too — add a section on AI spend approval thresholds and budget controls"
- "We need to address the Gemini vs. Claude question — when is free Gemini acceptable and when do we need Claude's capabilities?"

## Stretch Goals

- Build an interactive policy navigator — a web tool where employees select their function, use case, and data sensitivity, and get a tailored guidance page
- Create a quarterly AI governance review template the committee can use to track progress
- Draft the all-hands announcement email introducing the governance framework to the company
