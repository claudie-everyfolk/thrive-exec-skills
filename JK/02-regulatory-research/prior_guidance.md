## Memo 1: Comparative Advertising — FTC Compliance

**To:** Marketing Department
**From:** Legal — Associate Counsel (T. Okafor)
**Date:** December 12, 2025
**Re:** FTC Guidelines for Comparative Pricing Claims

### Summary

Marketing has asked whether Thrive Market can make direct price comparisons against named competitors (specifically Whole Foods Market and Sprouts Farmers Market) in advertising materials. This memo summarizes the applicable FTC framework.

### Analysis

The FTC's Comparative Advertising Policy Statement (1979) generally encourages comparative advertising as beneficial to consumers, provided claims are truthful and substantiated. Key requirements:

1. **Substantiation Standard**: Under the FTC's "reasonable basis" standard, advertisers must possess a reasonable basis for advertising claims before disseminating them. For objective claims (like price comparisons), this typically requires "competent and reliable evidence," which the FTC has interpreted as tests, analyses, research, or studies conducted and evaluated by qualified persons.

2. **Comparison Methodology**: Price comparisons must compare products of like grade and quality. The comparison must be based on the same or substantially similar products, not cherry-picked favorable comparisons.

3. **Timeliness of Data**: Price comparison data must be reasonably current. The FTC has not set a specific timeframe, but data older than six months is generally considered stale for grocery pricing given market volatility.

4. **Named Competitor References**: Under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)), comparative advertising that names a specific competitor is permissible but creates additional exposure to a false advertising claim brought directly by the named competitor.

5. **"Up to" and Average Claims**: Claims stating average savings must be substantiated by data showing the average is accurate and representative. The use of "average" is generally safer than "up to" claims, which the FTC scrutinizes more closely because the maximum savings may be achieved by very few consumers.

### Recommendations

- Thrive Market may make comparative pricing claims against named competitors provided the comparison methodology is documented, the data is current (within 90 days for grocery products), and the comparison uses identical or substantially equivalent products.
- Use "average" savings claims rather than "up to" claims. Ensure the sample size is statistically significant.
- Retain all supporting data and methodology documentation for a minimum of three years.
- Consider including a disclosure about comparison methodology (e.g., "Based on comparison of [X] products at Thrive Market vs. published online prices at [Competitor] as of [date]").

### Note on FTC Enforcement Trends

The FTC has been relatively inactive in comparative grocery advertising enforcement in recent years. The most relevant precedent remains the FTC's general guidance on substantiation. **We are not aware of any recent enforcement actions specifically targeting online grocery price comparisons.**

## Memo 2: Health and Wellness Claims in Food Marketing

**To:** Marketing Department & Content Team
**From:** Legal — Senior Counsel (M. Vasquez)
**Date:** August 3, 2025
**Re:** Regulatory Framework for Health-Adjacent Claims

### Summary

As Thrive Market expands its content marketing around health and wellness topics, this memo outlines the legal framework for making health-related claims in connection with food products.

### Analysis

**1. FDA vs. FTC Jurisdiction**

Food labeling is primarily regulated by the FDA, while food advertising is primarily regulated by the FTC. For online content that blurs the line between labeling and advertising (e.g., website product pages, blog content), both agencies may assert jurisdiction.

**2. Claim Categories**

The FDA recognizes three categories of claims for food products:
- **Health Claims**: Describe a relationship between a food substance and a disease/health condition. Require FDA pre-approval or must meet the "significant scientific agreement" standard.
- **Qualified Health Claims**: Supported by some scientific evidence but do not meet the significant scientific agreement standard. Must include qualifying language.
- **Structure/Function Claims**: Describe the role of a nutrient in maintaining normal body structure or function. Do not require FDA pre-approval but must be truthful and not misleading.

**3. "Organic = Healthier" Implications**

Making or implying that organic food is healthier than conventional food is a claim that requires substantiation. While USDA organic certification addresses production methods, it does not make health claims. Marketing that implies a direct health benefit from organic food consumption could be construed as an unsubstantiated health claim.

**4. Third-Party Study References**

Referencing published scientific studies in marketing content is generally permissible provided:
- The study is accurately described and not taken out of context
- The marketing does not overstate the study's conclusions
- Appropriate qualifiers are included (e.g., "one study suggests" rather than "studies prove")
- The study is published in a peer-reviewed journal

**The FTC's standard for referencing scientific studies is that the advertiser must have a "reasonable basis" for the claim, and a single study is generally insufficient to constitute a "reasonable basis" for a broad health claim.** However, referencing a specific study with appropriate context and qualification is different from making an independent health claim based on that study.

### Recommendations

- Avoid claims that directly state or imply organic food is healthier than conventional food.
- When referencing scientific studies, use qualifying language and cite the specific study rather than making general claims.
- Include disclaimers noting that Thrive Market is not making health claims and that individual results may vary.
- Content that discusses general wellness topics (e.g., "benefits of reducing processed food") is lower risk than content that links specific Thrive Market products to health outcomes.
- **All health-adjacent marketing content should be reviewed by legal before publication.**

### Important Caveat

This memo does not address state-specific advertising regulations. California, New York, and several other states have consumer protection statutes that may impose additional requirements beyond federal law. State-specific analysis should be conducted for any campaign with significant spend in those markets.
