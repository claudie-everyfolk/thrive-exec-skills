# Regulatory Reference — Key Provisions

*Compiled by Legal Ops for AI-assisted research. This file provides context for common regulatory frameworks referenced in Thrive Market marketing reviews. It is NOT a substitute for primary source verification.*

---

## 1. FTC Act — Section 5

**15 U.S.C. § 45(a)(1)**: "Unfair methods of competition in or affecting commerce, and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce, are hereby declared unlawful."

**Key Principles:**
- An advertisement is deceptive if it contains a misrepresentation or omission that is likely to mislead consumers acting reasonably under the circumstances, and the misrepresentation or omission is material.
- An advertisement is unfair if it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers that is not reasonably avoidable and not outweighed by countervailing benefits.
- The FTC requires that advertisers have a "reasonable basis" for claims before dissemination (the "substantiation doctrine").

---

## 2. FTC Comparative Advertising Policy Statement (1979)

**Core Position:** The FTC encourages the naming of, or reference to, competitors in advertising when the comparison is truthful and substantiated. The Commission has supported comparative advertising as a means of providing consumers with useful information.

**Key Requirements for Comparative Claims:**
- Claims must be substantiated by competent and reliable evidence
- Comparisons must be of products with similar attributes
- Comparisons should not convey false or misleading impressions even if each individual statement is literally true
- The overall impression of the ad matters, not just individual claims

## 3. FTC Substantiation Doctrine

**Pfizer Factors** (from In re Pfizer, Inc., 81 F.T.C. 23 (1972)):
When evaluating whether an advertiser has a "reasonable basis" for a claim, the FTC considers:
1. Type of claim (health/safety claims require higher substantiation)
2. Product involved
3. Consequences of a false claim
4. Benefits of a truthful claim
5. Cost of developing substantiation
6. Amount of substantiation experts would agree is reasonable

**For health and safety claims**, the FTC generally requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence," defined as: tests, analyses, research, or studies that have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by qualified persons and are generally accepted in the profession to yield accurate and reliable results.

## 4. California Business & Professions Code — Section 17500

**Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17500**: "It is unlawful for any person, firm, corporation or association, or any employee thereof with intent directly or indirectly to dispose of real or personal property or to perform services, professional or otherwise, or anything of any nature whatsoever or to induce the public to enter into any obligation relating thereto, to make or disseminate or cause to be made or disseminated before the public in this state... in any advertising device... any statement... which is untrue or misleading, and which is known, or which by the exercise of reasonable care should be known, to be untrue or misleading."

**Key Differences from Federal FTC Standard:**
- California's standard includes a "negligence" component — claims that *should have been known* to be misleading violate the statute, even without intent to deceive
- Private right of action available under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 (UCL) and § 17500
- California courts have been more aggressive than the FTC in pursuing food and grocery advertising claims
- Remedies include injunctive relief, restitution, and civil penalties up to $2,500 per violation

## 5. CCPA / CPRA — Consumer Data Use Provisions

**Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq.** (as amended by CPRA, effective January 1, 2023):

**Key Provisions Relevant to Marketing:**

- **Purpose Limitation (§ 1798.100(c))**: A business shall not collect or use personal information for purposes that are not reasonably necessary and proportionate to the purposes for which the personal information was collected or processed.

- **Notice at Collection (§ 1798.100(a))**: At or before the point of collection, businesses must inform consumers of the categories of personal information to be collected and the purposes for which it will be used.

- **Right to Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing (§ 1798.120)**: Consumers have the right to opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal information. "Sharing" includes making personal information available for cross-context behavioral advertising.

- **Sensitive Personal Information (§ 1798.121)**: Consumers have the right to limit the use and disclosure of sensitive personal information. Note: purchase history linked to an identifiable consumer may constitute personal information but is not classified as "sensitive" under CPRA.

- **Data Minimization (CPRA Regulations, 11 CCR § 7002)**: Businesses must collect, use, retain, and share personal information only as reasonably necessary and proportionate to the purposes disclosed in the privacy notice.

**Relevance to Marketing Analytics:**
- Using member purchase data to calculate aggregate savings claims (e.g., "average savings of $32/order") likely falls within the original collection purpose if the privacy notice disclosed use for "business analytics" or similar.
- Creating targeted audience segments from individual purchase histories for advertising purposes may constitute "sharing" if the segments are made available to third-party ad platforms.
- If Thrive Market's privacy notice does not explicitly disclose use of purchase data for advertising/marketing segmentation, this use may require a privacy notice update and/or opt-out opportunity.

## 6. NAD (National Advertising Division) Precedent — Grocery/Food

*Note: NAD decisions are self-regulatory and not legally binding, but they are influential and often cited by the FTC.*

The NAD has reviewed several cases involving grocery and food price comparison claims:
- Comparative price claims should be based on a "market basket" comparison (multiple products) rather than cherry-picked individual product comparisons
- The time period for price data should be disclosed
- Online prices vs. in-store prices should not be mixed in the same comparison without disclosure
- Membership-model pricing comparisons should disclose the cost of membership

## 7. USDA Organic Labeling

**7 CFR Part 205**: The USDA National Organic Program (NOP) regulates the use of the term "organic" in food labeling. USDA organic certification addresses agricultural production methods, not health outcomes. Advertising that implies organic food provides specific health benefits goes beyond the scope of USDA organic certification and may be subject to FTC/FDA scrutiny.
