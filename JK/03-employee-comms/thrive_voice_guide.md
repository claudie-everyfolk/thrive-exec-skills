# Voice of Thrive — Internal Communications Style Guide

**Version:** 1.0
**Created:** June 2026
**Owner:** People Ops + Internal Communications

## Our Voice in One Sentence

We talk to Thrivers the way a trusted team lead talks to their team: directly, honestly, and with genuine care for the humans on the other side.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Lead with the "Why," Not the "What"

Don't start with the policy. Start with the reason. People can accept hard news when they understand the reasoning behind it. They resist when they feel like something is being done *to* them.

**Do:** "We've been hearing from managers and new hires that it's harder to build relationships and move projects forward when we're not together. That feedback — combined with what we're seeing in our product development timelines — led us to rethink how we work."

**Don't:** "Effective October 1, 2026, all HQ employees are required to be in the office a minimum of 3 days per week."

### 2. Acknowledge the Impact

Never pretend a change doesn't affect people. If it does, say so. If it's inconvenient, say that too. Thrivers can tell when we're spin-doctoring, and it erodes trust faster than the bad news itself.

**Do:** "We know this is a real change, and for some of you, it means rethinking routines you've built over the past two years."

**Don't:** "We're excited to announce an enhanced in-office collaboration framework."

### 3. Be Direct, Not Corporate

Say it plainly. Use short sentences. Avoid jargon, legalese, and corporate euphemism. If you wouldn't say it in a conversation, don't write it in an email.

**Do:** "We're switching dental providers."

**Don't:** "We are transitioning to a new dental benefits ecosystem partner."

### 4. Use "Thrivers," Not "Employees"

Our people are Thrivers. It's not just branding — it reflects how we think about the team. Use "Thrivers" in communications, "team" in casual contexts, and "employees" only in legal/compliance documents where formality is required.

### 5. Show, Don't Just Tell

When something is a genuine positive, let the facts speak. Don't oversell. When something is hard, show what you're doing about it — don't just acknowledge and move on.

**Do:** "To make this transition easier, we're offering a $250 provider transition stipend for any Thriver whose dentist isn't in the new network."

**Don't:** "We're committed to supporting you through this transition."

## Phrases to Use

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---|---|
| "We are pleased to announce" | "Here's what's happening" / "Quick update" |
| "Effective immediately" | "Starting [date]" |
| "Please be advised" | (just say the thing) |
| "Human capital" | "Our team" / "Thrivers" |
| "Leverage" (as a verb) | "Use" |
| "Align" / "Alignment" | "Work together" / "Get on the same page" |
| "Synergy" | (never use this word) |
| "Circle back" | "Follow up" |
| "Touch base" | "Check in" |
| "At this time" | "Right now" / "Today" |
| "Going forward" | "From now on" / "Starting [date]" |
| "Best-in-class" | (be specific about what's actually good) |
| "It has come to our attention" | (just say what happened) |
| "We regret to inform you" | "Unfortunately" / "Here's the hard part" |

## Formatting Guidelines

### Email Communications

- **Subject lines:** Clear and specific. No clickbait, no ALL CAPS, no exclamation marks for serious topics. One exclamation mark is okay for genuinely good news.
- **Length:** Under 400 words for announcements. Link to detailed FAQ or wiki for details.
- **Structure:** Context first (1-2 sentences of "why"), then the news, then what it means for the reader, then next steps.
- **Sign-off:** Use the name of the most relevant leader (CEO for company-wide, VP People for benefits, etc.) — not "The People Team" or "HR."

### Slack Messages

- **Shorter than email.** 2-4 sentences max for the main message. Thread the details.
- **Use emoji sparingly** and only for positive or neutral content. No 🎉 on a benefits change announcement.
- **Pin important messages** in relevant channels.

### Fulfillment Center Communications

- **Simple language.** Assume the reader has 30 seconds and is reading on a phone or a break room poster.
- **Bullet points over paragraphs.**
- **Include dates, deadlines, and action items up front.**
- **Translate to Spanish** for sites where >20% of the workforce is Spanish-speaking (currently: Batesville FC).
- **Manager talking points** are mandatory for any change that affects FC operations, schedules, or compensation/benefits.

## Tone by Situation

| Situation | Tone |
|---|---|
| **Good news** | Warm, celebratory, grounded — proud but not boastful |
| **Neutral change** | Clear, matter-of-fact, helpful |
| **Difficult change** | Honest, empathetic, direct — lead with why, acknowledge impact, show action |
| **Crisis** | Calm, factual, accountable — no spin, frequent updates |
| **Policy enforcement** | Firm but fair — explain the standard and why it matters |

---

## The Mission Connection

Thrive Market's mission is to make healthy living accessible to everyone. Internal communications should reflect that same commitment to accessibility:
- Don't assume everyone has a desk, a laptop, or constant email access
- Don't use acronyms without defining them
- Don't reference HQ-centric experiences as if they're universal (not everyone has a "commute" — some Thrivers walk to the FC floor)
- When a change affects FC and HQ differently, acknowledge that directly

## Review Process

| Sensitivity | Review Required |
|---|---|
| Low (celebrations, reminders) | People Ops manager approval |
| Medium (policy clarifications, benefits info) | VP People review |
| High (benefits changes, org changes) | VP People + Legal review |
| Very High (layoffs, RTO, compensation changes) | CEO + GC/CAO + VP People review |
